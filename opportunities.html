Opportunities

In an ever-changing world, almost anything is possible to produce with the help of technology which goes the same for Artificial Intelligence (AI) Robots in surgical health care. As of now the role of robotic surgery in the operation room is a computer-controlled device designed to assist with the manipulation and positioning of surgical instruments, enabling the surgeon to dedicate all his/her attention to then intricate parts of the surgery. Offering unparalleled control in terms of speed and movement, minimizing hand tremors, and avoiding accidental hand movements. This gives surgeons to operate with increased flexibility, precision, agility, and greater control, especially during surgeries that are physically. Today’s surgical robot AI is restricted to image recognition algorithm for pre-operative planning, however the opportunity for AI to be integrated with Surgical Robots is limitless.

At this point, we ponder the question “Sure, that sounds cool, but will it be practical, and will it work in real organizations?”. Artificial Intelligence into surgery was introduced more recently, however with a strong root in imaging, navigation, and early techniques focus on feature detection and computer-assisted intervention for both preoperative planning and intraoperative guidance. Previously, the first surgical robots were the Arthrobot, which had built in voice commands to assist in patient positioning during an orthopedic case. In 1996, the Automated Endoscopic System for Optimal Positioning (AESOP), was able to obtain the first FDA-approved robotic surgical system (Zemmar et al, 2020), called ZEUS. Which led to the first long-distance tele-surgical procedure on a patient in Strasbourg, while the surgeon was in New York. Impressive for the technology they were presented during their time. In New Zealand, the Ministry of Health, provided a list of robots that can be used within the Health System, wherein Surgical Robots are included. In fact, in 2007, the first robotic surgery was first performed at Grace Hospital, Tauranga, where surgeon Peter Giling performed a robotic assisted prostatectomy for a patient with prostate cancer (Ministry of Health, 2019). Additionally, according to New Zealand’s Ministry of Health, there are currently three Da Vinci Robots, which specialises in translating the surgeon’s hand movements into smaller and precise movements.

AI surgical will come to play, especially in the age of COVID-19 where it has caused us to distant from each other, which brings a potential threat to surgeons and nurses working in clinical and hospitals. To effectively reduce, the spread, AI robotic surgery can be integrated at several sections of the sequence, which each surgical patient traverses during a hospital stay. Allowing for the workflow to be divided into pre-operative care, anesthesia, the surgical procedure and postoperative care, the overall goal of this is to minimize contact between patient and healthcare provider at each step. AI can be the next frontier for surgical robots, the Smart Tissue Autonomous Robot (STAR) a powered by AI, used to detect tissue motion (Leonard et al, 2014), in one trial it was able to successfully sew pig intestine for diagnostic purposes gave an insight into the use of AI in surgical robotics. Giving us an insight, the benefit of completely automated robots is well worth the investment. Interest in AI robotic surgery is rising due to the fact they bring in multiple benefits. One of which is the manpower planning, as doctor-patient ratio is steadily declining, and robots are a great way to tackle that. Another is they will be a great help in assisting surgeons in micro-surgical procedures, even though it is still a far-fetched concept, AI surgical robot can provide valuable, real-time inputs to enable surgeons to perform better. However, the design of an AI is just as important as the design of the surgical robot itself as AI allows the robot to work unsupervised which needs to be very well designed due to the stakes at hand.

With an AI-based algorithm and the combination with the precision and control of surgical robots will revolutionize the way humans look at surgeries in this age. The combination of this will provide the ability to simplify the medium of interaction between surgical robots and surgeons with the help of AI. It allows itself to recognize and determine the movements and patterns of a surgeon during surgery and possibly convert them into actionable commands for the robot, in other words the AI will collect data over time by watching surgeons perform. With the assistance of collection data and algorithms, AI will have a huge contribution in assisting surgical robots with reasoning and performing of cognitive functions like decision-making, problem-solving, speech-recognition.
